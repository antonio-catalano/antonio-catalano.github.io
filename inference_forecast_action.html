Inference, forecast
and action in the Quantitative Era

Posted by Antonio Catalano on October 7th, 2020
Don't play what's there, play what's not there
Miles Davis

The Quantitative space


Data are means of transportation.
Models and tools are the infrastructure.
The analyst/modeler is the driver.

  • If the quality of data is good we have reliable means of transportation.
  • If models and tools are powerful and robust we have a stable infrastructure.
  • If the captain is skilled and careful we have a wise driver.


But an overlooked question still remains: which is the destination?

The Good, the Bad and the Ugly


What does the Sergio Leone's masterpiece have in common with our topic?
Almost nothing, but given the fact that our story has three virtual characters, why not?
Anyway, let's get serious now.

Inference is the arrival station of the truth town.
Forecast is the arrival station of the functional town.
Action is the arrival station of the fitness town.

Those are three different destinations.

Inference means that through data we get to either understand the nature of a hidden process or the behaviour of a variable or the properties of a dynamics.
Most of the time we didn't actually get to understand the hidden object, but we believe we had it all figured out.
It doesn't make a big difference: the main point is that we believe in an unveiled correspondence between the subject (the inner world of the perceiver) and the object (its outer world).
And, curiously, everyone thinks they have the true correspondence once the inference is made.
As a matter of fact it doesn't make sense to talk about a false correspondence: the notions of correspondence and truth are intimately related.
Inference is where idealism seeks to be friends with materialism.

Forecast instead doesn't care about that at all.
Forecast simply tries to anticipate a future outcome, without caring about which is the path that returns that outcome.
It is not indolent, it simply admits that it can't grasp the dynamic cause-effect series that brings to the outcome.
Forecast is a bizarre character in our plot: it wants to anticipate a future chapter or sentence of the book (i.e. the model's output) by reading only the current unfinished book and creating the remaining part.
In other words forecast knows that it is not able to explain why the reality behaves how it does, but neverthless, for whatever reasons, it makes a bet on the dress it will wear.

Action is a forecast character with a survival payoff constraint.
It is much less lighthearted than the forecast character and far less cocky than the inference one.
Its aim is not so noble as the inference's aim: in fact it is not searching for why and how.
Its aim is not so ego-driven as the forecast's one: it is not only about to be right.
Action wants to maximize a survival payoff: and in order to do that it has not only to guess what the future will be like, but, mainly, what will happen if this and what if that.
Action has not time nor energy to think about the truth, and no privilege allows him to only be right.
It has to arrive to the next day in the best possible conditions.

Philosophy, science and power


Statistical inference represents a continuation of the quest begun with the greek philosophy.
The main difference is that statistical inference places itself within a very different cultural atmosphere compared to that in which Thales of Miletus, Heraclitus, Socrates and all their sons abided.
Statistical inference doesn't aim to reach the episteme: in fact it doesn't even believe that an episteme exists.
Nonetheless, every time we pronounce a claim about the functioning of the reality (or of a part of it) we are subconsciously assuming that some sort of truth (maybe spatio-temporal conditioned, i.e. not incontrovertible regardless space and time) exists.
Inference,Truth and Episteme are very related concepts, and only by looking them directly in the eyes we understand how problematic is every inference-type claim.
I would say that the immediate reply someone could make is "hey man, I only want to find the coefficients of this regression in order to explain the relationship between X and Y". Or "hey man, too much intellectualism: we inferential analysts only want to know how a certain part of the reality behaves in absence of deterministic patterns: through data, models, tools we aim to make some induction about that".
Nothing actually changes in the deep chambers of the discourse.
Inference can't exist without the existence of the concept of truth .
When we are making inferences, we are making epistemology, whatever we know it or not.
But without knowing it we would likely make bad inferences.

I used a small trick in the previous sentences.
In fact Statistical Inference (S.I.) is not exactly about Truth.
Truth in philosophical and religious traditions is the goal.
It represents un unconditioned concept, not subordinated to concepts like empirical verification or falsifiability: in fact it is the unconditioned concept.
In this sense Science, and hence S.I., is much more humble but at the same time much more severe.
The truth involved in Science and in S.I. is a truth without capital T.
Its real name is Explanatory Power.
While philosophy and religions want to tell us what the world is, science wants to tell us how the world behaves.
Statistical Inference is about giving the most powerful explanation in a stochastic territory, full of fog.
But is actually exist the most powerful explanation in a given situation? And who is able to detect it?
These question seem to beg the existence of something deeper.
Can this something deeper be something different from the concept of Truth? It seems not.
Or the Explanatory Power has no dependence, no connection with the concept of Truth in a sort of chaotic simulation where it is what it is and that's all?

The biological world


When we work with data in a field related with a certain degree to the biological realm, the Inference character can easily resamble an exhausted war veteran.
The complexity of the object of study almost always overwhelms the human ability to carry data to that destination.
The Forecast character does better, provided that it maintains a certain dose of humulity.
The real star of this scene, as you can imagine, is the Action character.
This is something that many data scientists, economists, analysts, may gloss over but it is a crucial point.
Every time the quantitative framework is aimed for a biology-related field (psychology, economics, finance, social disciplines in general) we have to have in mind that the destination is neither the Inference nor the Forecast: it is the Action.
The studying/modelling process and the related considerations have to take into account this fact.
And, finally, if the Action is the destination of each quantitative study related to biological domains, isn't it true that data are not sufficient to make a good job?
If we evolved until now without statistics and software, isn't it true that, in name of rigourous analysis we often miss the aim of the analysis, so to speak, how to overcome the obstacle with a minimum cost and in a timely way?

In the mismatching Era of the quantitative dictate used by and for humans with the same biological genes of thousand of years ago, we should not overlook the importance of intuitive heuristics.
